# Design jam with Priya

Recorded Mar 20, 2026
Participants: [[Priya Kapoor]], me

## Notes

### Key ideas
- The 2D canvas metaphor is overused. Outlines are too rigid. Graphs are beautiful but unusable for real work.
- Priya's provocation: what if the primary interface is conversational, and the spatial view is secondary?
- Reframing [[Elena Voss]]'s "knowledge neighborhoods" — proximity isn't about graph position, it's about contextual relevance. Two notes are neighbors when they're useful at the same time.

### Three interface modes (sketched)
1. **Conversation** — ask and explore (default)
2. **Feed** — what changed and why it matters (new idea)
3. **Map** — spatial overview for orientation (used sparingly)

## Transcript

Priya Kapoor: So here's my problem with everything in this space right now. Everyone's doing 2D canvases. Outlines are too rigid. Graphs look amazing in demos but nobody actually works in them.

Me: What's the alternative?

Priya Kapoor: What if the primary interface isn't visual at all? What if it's conversational — you talk to your vault — and the spatial view is something you pull up when you need the big picture. Conversation is the default mode.

Me: That maps to something Elena Voss has been writing about — knowledge neighborhoods.

Priya Kapoor: Right, but I'd reframe it. She talks about proximity in a graph. I think neighborhoods aren't about spatial position, they're about contextual relevance. Two notes are neighbors when they're useful at the same time, regardless of topic.

Me: So the neighborhood is defined by the task, not the content.

Priya Kapoor: Exactly. Which gives you three modes. One: conversation — ask and explore, that's the default. Two: feed — what changed and why it matters. Not a dashboard, a feed. Three: map — the spatial overview, but only when you need orientation.

Me: The feed idea is interesting. Where does that come from?

Priya Kapoor: James Okafor's community keeps saying the same thing — people don't want dashboards, they want awareness. A feed gives you that. It's the same principle I'm applying to the developer portal for Sarah's team. Developers want to know what changed in the API, not stare at a docs site.

Me: Would you be willing to review our current UI and propose something?

Priya Kapoor: Yeah, I'd want to talk to Marco first though. Understanding the investor lens on UX differentiation matters — I don't want to propose something beautiful that doesn't sell.
